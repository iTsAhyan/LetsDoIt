February 25th, 1957. Philadelphia. The woods off Susquehanna Road are cold, damp, and silent. 
    The kind of morning where fog hangs low and the city feels a thousand miles away. 
    A 26-year-old college student named Frederick Benonis is alone in the brush. 
    He's not here for a walk. He's checking his rabbit traps. They're illegal, but he's a student. 
    He needs the money. He pushes through a thicket of weeds. His boot hits something solid. 
    He looks down. It's a large cardboard box. J.C. Penney. The kind used to ship a baby bassinet. 
    It's out of place. Soggy from the dew. He kneels. He lifts the cardboard flaps. And his life stops. 
    Inside, curled on his side in the fetal position, is the body of a small boy. 
    He's naked. He's clean. His eyes are closed. He looks like he's sleeping. 
    But Frederick knows immediately. This child is not sleeping. 
    The boy can't be more than four to six years old. He's three feet tall. But his ribs are visible. 
    He weighs only thirty pounds. His dark hair has been hacked off. Cruelly. Unevenly. 
    Chopped close to the scalp with what looks like scissors. His body is covered in bruises. 
    Yellow ones, purple ones, fresh ones. His knuckles are scraped raw. Defensive wounds. 
    The box is otherwise empty. No blanket. No clothing. No toy. Just a child. Discarded like garbage. 
    Within the hour, the woods are filled with men. Philadelphia Police Department. Detectives. 
    The Medical Examiner. The FBI. They photograph the box. They photograph the boy. 
    They photograph the trees, the dirt, the fog. They have a body. But that's all they have. 
    No missing child report matches his description. His fingerprints aren't on file anywhere in the country. 
    No one has reported a boy missing. He's given a case number: 57-2196. 
    But the city gives him a different name. The Boy in the Box. 
    The next day, the autopsy is performed. The medical examiner's report is three pages long. 
    And every line is worse than the last. Cause of death: Blunt force trauma to the head. 
    Multiple, separate impacts. This was not an accident. He was beaten to death. 
    But the abuse started long before that day. The boy was severely malnourished. 
    His stomach was completely empty. His esophagus was scarred from chronic, violent vomiting. 
    He had never received a single vaccination. He had never been to a dentist. 
    His teeth were undeveloped. He had never been to school. 
    For four years, this child existed in complete darkness. Hidden from the world. 
    The investigation becomes the largest in Philadelphia history. 
    They create a post-mortem death mask of the boy's face. They print it. 
    They put that photo in every single gas bill mailed to every home in Philadelphia. 
    They put it on post office walls. They broadcast it on national television. 
    A plea goes out across the country: "Do you know this boy?" 
    The calls come in. Hundreds of them. Desperate parents from every state. 
    "That's my son. He was kidnapped last summer." "That's my grandson. We haven't seen him in two years." 
    Detectives follow every lead. They drive to Ohio. They drive to Florida. 
    They dig up backyards based on anonymous tips. Every single lead is a dead end. 
    One theory claims he was the son of a carnival worker. The FBI spends months tracking carnivals. Nothing. 
    A woman from a foster home calls police. She claims her foster mother bought the boy and abused him. 
    Detectives investigate the home for two years. The woman is eventually declared mentally unstable. 
    Another dead end. A psychic insists the boy was a Hungarian refugee. 
    Immigration services check every refugee child. No match. The case goes cold. 
    But it never leaves the public consciousness. In 1998, the boy is exhumed from his grave in Potter's Field. 
    Forensic scientists extract his DNA, hoping 21st-century technology can solve a 1950s crime. 
    His DNA goes into CODIS, the national database. There are no matches. 
    He is reburied under a new headstone, paid for by a grieving city. 
    The stone reads: "America's Unknown Child. Known Only To God." 
    The file sits for twenty-four more years. Until 2022. 
    A non-profit team of genetic genealogists takes the boy's DNA profile. 
    This is the same technology that caught the Golden State Killer. 
    They upload his DNA to public genealogy databases. They don't get a name. 
    They get cousins. Third cousins. Fourth cousins. Thousands of them. And they start building. 
    They build a family tree up, finding common ancestors from the 1800s. 
    They use census records, obituaries, marriage licenses. Then they build it back down. 
    Through every branch of the family. It's painstaking work. It takes months. 
    And then, after sixty-five years, a name emerges. Joseph Augustus Zarelli. 
    Born January 13th, 1953, at St. Joseph's Hospital in Philadelphia. 
    His mother was twenty-one years old. She was unmarried. In 1953, that was a scandal that could destroy a family's reputation. 
    She gave him up for adoption directly from the hospital. 
    The couple who took him home were named Martha and Augustine. They lived in a row house in West Philadelphia. 
    After the identification, detectives found the neighbors. Now elderly, they remembered Martha and Augustine. 
    They remembered something else, too. They remembered hearing a child. Crying. Screaming. 
    At all hours of the night. The sound came from the basement. 
    But they never, in four years, ever saw a child at that house. 
    Joseph was never enrolled in school. The Philadelphia School District has no record of him. 
    He never saw a doctor. There are no medical records. He lived in that basement. 
    The evidence suggests he was beaten regularly. He was starved. 
    The crudely cut hair found on his body? The medical examiner now believes it was done after he died, to hide head wounds. 
    When Joseph finally died from his injuries in February of 1957, Martha and Augustine panicked. 
    They cleaned his body. They wrapped him in a blanket they later disposed of. 
    They found the J.C. Penney bassinet box in their house. 
    And in the middle of the night, they drove to the woods off Susquehanna Road. And they left him there. 
    Martha died in 1991. Augustine died years before her. They were never suspects. 
    Their names never appeared in the original case file. They were never questioned. 
    They were never arrested. They never spent a single day in prison. 
    On December 8th, 2022, the Philadelphia Police Department officially closed the case. 
    They gave the Boy in the Box his name back. Joseph Augustus Zarelli now has a headstone with his real name. 
    He has a birth certificate. He has a place in his family's history. 
    But the file is closed for a different reason. There is no one left to prosecute. 
    After sixty-five years, Joseph has his identity. But Joseph Augustus Zarelli never got justice. 
    The people who tortured him, who starved him, who beat a four-year-old child to death... 
    they died in their beds. Free. Case closed.
